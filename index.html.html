Introduction

In America, motor vehicle collisions are one of the leading causes of death. When one enters the roads of America, whether they are behind the wheel or not, the risks are often overlooked. Using the roads is a modern-day necessity, however, many people are unaware of how to mitigate risks. By analyzing traffic accident data, our team hopes to identify: "What are key motor vehicle collision risk factors that everyday people can avoid?" With this knowledge, drivers can make educated decisions to protect themselves and others on the road.

Data Curation

For this project, we will be using the dataset from City of New York's Local Government Data on Motor Vehicle Collisions - Crashes, which we've saved as "Motor_Vehicles_Collisions.csv." This file contains police-reported motor vehicle collisions in NYC involving injuries or deaths starting from 2014 to the present day.

Imports
The main library we are using is Pandas, an open-source software library for data manipulation and analysis.

We also widely used SciPy for statistical functions for data analysis.

Primary analysis

Our question was, "What are key motor vehicle collision risk factors that everyday people can avoid?" To best answer this question we decided to use a Logistic Regression machine learning technique. This technique will help classify collisions based on severity, location, and time of day. Then with our data classified into such understandable factors we can draw conclusions on major risk factors and how people can avoid them. For example, if it seems rush hour correlates to a significant increase in severe crashes we can suggest rush hour as a key risk factor and recommend people stay off roads during rush hour whenever possible to avoid it.

In[37]:
# Loading dataset
crashes_df = pd.read_csv('Motor_Vehicles_Collisions.csv', low_memory=False)

# Converting dates and times
crashes_df['CRASH DATE'] = pd.to_datetime(crashes_df['CRASH DATE'], errors='coerce')
crashes_df['CRASH TIME'] = pd.to_datetime(crashes_df['CRASH TIME'], errors='coerce', format='%H:%M')
crashes_df['CRASH HOUR'] = crashes_df['CRASH TIME'].dt.hour
crashes_df['DAY_OF_WEEK'] = crashes_df['CRASH DATE'].dt.dayofweek
crashes_df['IS_WEEKEND'] = crashes_df['DAY_OF_WEEK'] >= 5
crashes_df['IS_RUSH_HOUR'] = crashes_df['CRASH HOUR'].between(6, 9) | crashes_df['CRASH HOUR'].between(15, 18)
# ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ #


# For Machine Learning, We will use Logistic Regression
from sklearn.linear_model import LogisticRegression
from sklearn.model_selection import train_test_split

# Binary Classification - 1 if persons were injured or killed. 0 if not.
crashes_df['SEVERE'] = (
    (crashes_df['NUMBER OF PERSONS INJURED'].fillna(0) > 0) |
    (crashes_df['NUMBER OF PERSONS KILLED'].fillna(0) > 0)
).astype(int)


# Drop rows with missing boroughs
crashes_df = crashes_df.dropna(subset=['BOROUGH'])


# Prepare features for model
features = ['CRASH HOUR', 'DAY_OF_WEEK', 'IS_WEEKEND', 'IS_RUSH_HOUR', 'BOROUGH']
X = crashes_df[features].copy()
X = pd.get_dummies(X, columns=['BOROUGH'])
y = crashes_df['SEVERE']

# Train / test split
# 75% training and 25% test:
X_train, X_test, y_train, y_test = train_test_split(X, y, test_size=0.25, random_state=42)

# Fit the model
model = LogisticRegression(class_weight='balanced', max_iter=1000)
model.fit(X_train, y_train)


# Print severe data
print(crashes_df['SEVERE'].value_counts(normalize=True))
# ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ #

print(classification_report(y_test, y_pred))

Insights and Conclusions

Insights:

Early morning (12 AM - 6 AM)

  • Manhattan, Queens, Staten Island - These 3 boroughs appear to have decreasing severe accident proportions before they increase.
  • Brooklyn, Brox - These 2 boroughs have stable but slightly decreasing severe accident proportions.

Morning rush (6 AM - 9 AM)

  • Staten Island - There is a significant increase in the proportion of severe accidents proportions until 7 am before sharply declining.
  • Brooklyn, Queens - There is a slight increase in the proportion of severe accidents proportions until 7 am before consistently declining.
  • Brox, Manhattan - There is a consistent decline in the proportion of severe accidents proportions.

Working times (9 AM - 3 PM)

  • Staten Island - There is a decrease in the proportion of severe accidents until 10 am, after which it consistently increases.
  • Brooklyn, Queens, Bronx, Manhattan There is a decrease in the proportion of severe accidents until 10 am, after which it consistently increases.

Afternoon rush(3 PM - 6 PM)

  • All Broughs - There is a local peak in the proportion of severe accidents around 3 PM, after which it decreases until 4 PM, after which it consistently increases.

Night time (6 PM - 12 AM)

  • Brooklyn, Bronx, Staten Island - The proportion of severe accidents increases until 9 PM before decreasing until 10 PM and increasing again.
  • Manhattan - The proportion of severe accidents increases until 9 PM, before it decreases.
  • Queens - The proportion of severe accidents increases until 8 PM before it stabilizes.

Overall

  • The morning rush (6-9 AM) generally shows a very minor spike in severe accidents before a sharp
  • The evening rush (3-6 PM) tends to coincide with an increase in the proportion of severe crashes, particularly in Brooklyn, Bronx, and Queens.
  • This suggests that the evening rush hour may be a higher risk window in terms of crash severity.
  • 9 AM - 2 PM appears to be the safest time to be on the roads, while 8 PM - 7 AM is the most dangerous
  • All Broughs have the same general trend from 6 AM to 9 PM, which suggests that something like rush hour affects everyone.

Conclusions:

  • We can see that testing the crash data using time (rush hour vs non-rush hour) and location (by borough) provided evidence that time of day and location both influence the risk of injury or death.

  • By combining exploratory data analysis with machine learning, we reached several key insights:

    1. Rush hour crashes are statistically more likely to result in injuries or fatalities.
    1. The time of day and borough have significance in crash severity.
    1. Our logistic regression model, trained on these features, was able to predict whether a crash would be severe with reasonable accuracy and provided interpretable weights for each factor.
    1. Brooklyn, Staten Island, and the Bronx were the boroughs with consistently higher proportions of severe crashes during rush hour.
    1. Manhattan had consistently the lowest proportions despite having the highest population, suggesting that low vehicle speeds played a role.

  • Our analysis set out to answer a straightforward question: "What are key motor vehicle collision risk factors that everyday people can avoid?" Or more specifically, "What factors increase the likelihood that a motor vehicle crash will result in injury or death? With that information, could we create actionable insights to reduce the proportion of severe crashes?"

  • Now to answer the question: it seems night driving, sunrise driving, sunset driving, the afternoon rush, and locations with higher vehicle speeds played a role.

  • Actionable Insights:

    1. It is best to stay off the roads during sunrises, sunsets, and nighttime.
    1. Drivers should be better educated on how to handle driving during times when the amount of daylight is low or changing.
    1. It is best to stay off the roads during the afternoon rush.
    1. There should be heightened emergency service presence during the most dangerous times.
    1. Lower speeds help lower the proportion of severe collisions.
    1. Try to commute from 9 AM - 2 PM.
